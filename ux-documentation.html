Author's Note
Preface
The idea for this project arrived not in a studio, but in a wardrobe room — specifically, in the chaos of a luxury residential wardrobe that had been meticulously sourced by an interior designer but never properly organised, catalogued, or communicated to the client who owned it. The garments were extraordinary. The experience of arriving at them was not.
Interior designers who specialise in residential wardrobe curation occupy a quiet but demanding niche. They manage wardrobes the way architects manage space — with deliberate intention about proportion, adjacency, occasion, and material. And yet the digital tools available to them remain firmly in the realm of spreadsheets, email threads, and WhatsApp groups. The gap between the quality of the output and the quality of the process struck me as significant — and as a design problem worth solving.
This project, Studio Materium: A Wardrobe Intelligence System for Interior Designers, is my attempt to close that gap. It proposes a purpose-built platform that gives designers a structured digital workflow, gives clients meaningful visibility into their own wardrobe's construction, and gives studio administrators the operational overview they need to manage multiple concurrent projects without friction.
The visual language of the platform is deliberately luxurious — dark, editorial, architectural — because the users it serves have high expectations of everything they interact with, including their professional tools. A wardrobe intelligence system should feel as considered as the wardrobes it helps create.
This document traces the full design journey: from research and problem definition, through ideation and system design, to a complete set of high-fidelity interface screens, a working prototype, and tested user flows. I hope it reads as both a rigorous design process and a coherent argument for why luxury professional tools deserve luxury design.
— Damini Sirohi, May 2026

Chapter I
Intro-
duction
"The luxury wardrobe is no longer just storage. It is a curated expression of identity — and the designers who create them deserve digital tools worthy of that ambition."
The luxury residential interior market is undergoing a quiet transformation. High-net-worth clients are increasingly commissioning bespoke wardrobes as considered extensions of their living spaces — places of ritual, order, and self-expression rather than mere utility. Interior designers who specialise in this space operate at the intersection of fashion knowledge, spatial planning, and personal styling.
Yet despite the sophistication of the output, the process behind it remains fragmented. Designers coordinate across emails, spreadsheets, and messaging apps. Clients receive little visibility until the final reveal. Studio managers chase updates manually. The tools do not match the craft.
This project — Studio Materium: A Wardrobe Intelligence System for Interior Designers — proposes a purpose-built digital platform that brings structure, transparency, and luxury visual language to the full wardrobe curation workflow. It is designed for three distinct roles: Admin, Designer, and Client, each with a dashboard and set of capabilities calibrated to their specific needs within the process.

III · 01
Project Context
The luxury residential interior market in India is growing at an estimated 8.3% CAGR through 2030, driven by a rising population of ultra-high-net-worth individuals who are investing significantly in the design and curation of their living environments. Within this market, bespoke wardrobe design has emerged as a high-value service category — one that demands specialist knowledge spanning garment types, materials, occasion-based curation, care protocols, and spatial planning.
Interior designers who specialise in wardrobe curation typically manage between five and fifteen residential projects simultaneously. Each project involves an extended collaboration with the client — from an initial briefing session through multiple rounds of selection, review, and refinement — that can span three to six months. During this time, the designer is responsible for maintaining a coherent vision across dozens or hundreds of individual garment and accessory selections.
The challenge is not in the knowledge or the craft — Studio Materium's designers are deeply skilled — but in the workflow. The process of coordinating selections, obtaining client feedback, managing approvals, and tracking project status is conducted across a patchwork of tools: spreadsheets for item lists, email for brief documentation, WhatsApp for client communication, and verbal updates for internal coordination.
"The quality of the wardrobe was never the problem. The quality of the process to get there was."

III · 02
Problem Area
Problem Statement
"Interior designers managing high-end residential wardrobes lack a dedicated digital system that combines workflow efficiency, client collaboration, and luxury visual presentation — resulting in fragmented processes, poor client visibility, and inconsistent outcomes."
The problem is multi-layered. At the operational level, the absence of a shared digital workspace means that every piece of information — client brief, item selections, approval status, designer notes — exists in a different location and format. This creates an administrative overhead that consumes a significant portion of every designer's working week.
At the client experience level, the fragmented process means that clients have no visibility into their wardrobe as it is being built. They receive updates reactively, via informal channels, and often cannot accurately recall what was agreed, approved, or flagged in previous conversations. The final wardrobe reveal becomes a high-stakes moment when it should be the confirmation of a well-understood shared vision.
Five Core Pain Points
01 — Fragmented Communication
Project information dispersed across email, WhatsApp, spreadsheets, and verbal discussions. No single source of truth. Status tracking is manual and unreliable.
02 — No Client Visibility
Clients receive no proactive updates during the configuration process. They have no way to track progress or engage with individual item selections before the final presentation.
03 — Unstructured Feedback
Client feedback is received verbally or via informal messages, with no structured connection to individual items. Designers cannot respond to specific flags in context.
04 — Admin Overhead
Studio managers manually chase status updates from every designer. Reports are compiled by hand. No real-time operational overview exists.
05 — No Garment Intelligence
Care, storage, and preservation information is not systematically communicated to clients. This reduces the value perceived from the service and leads to garment damage over time.

Scope & Why This Project
Scope of the Project
This project encompasses the full UX and UI design of the Studio Materium Wardrobe Configuration Portal — from research and problem definition through information architecture, user flows, design system, high-fidelity UI screens, and interactive prototype.
Included: Three role-based web application dashboards; wardrobe builder catalogue; zone-based configuration; item-level review and approval; care & storage intelligence; notification system; design system; interactive Figma prototype.
Excluded: Back-end development (API, database); payment integration; mobile app; AI recommendation engine; third-party brand API integration. These are noted as future scope.
Platform: Web-first responsive application. Primary screen sizes: 1440px desktop (primary), 1280px (secondary). Tablet and mobile layouts considered but not the primary design focus.
"This is a design project, not a development project — but it is designed as if it will be built."
Why This Project
Three reasons brought me to this project specifically.
First, the gap is genuine and under-served. Every professional workflow tool I surveyed during the early research phase was built for fashion retail, personal styling, or generic project management — none was designed for the specific collaborative workflow of a luxury interior design practice managing client wardrobes. The opportunity to design for an authentic, unmet need is rare at student level.
Second, it demands real design complexity. A three-role system with distinct permissions, a hierarchical catalogue, multiple project statuses, and a care-intelligence layer is not a simple product to design. It requires genuine information architecture thinking, not just visual craft — and that complexity is what I wanted my graduation project to demonstrate.
Third, the luxury context demanded visual ambition. Designing for users who expect excellence — in everything they interact with — pushed me to a level of typographic and compositional precision that a more utilitarian brief would not have required. The constraint of luxury became a creative driver.
The actual system was built in React 18 + TypeScript and deployed on Vercel — giving this project both a complete design artefact and a working implementation, which is unusual for a graduate project.

IV · 01
Luxury Wardrobe Systems
The contemporary luxury wardrobe has evolved far beyond its historical function as a storage unit. For high-net-worth clients, the wardrobe is now understood as a curated system — one that must account for garment preservation, occasion-based organisation, material adjacency, and visual display as much as functional storage capacity.
Research into leading luxury wardrobe installations — including those by Dressing Rooms by MOFAD, California Closets' luxury division, and European bespoke joinery specialists — reveals a consistent set of spatial and organisational principles: full-length and half-length hanging rails calibrated to garment type; drawered systems for accessories and delicate folded items; shoe racks designed to accommodate seasonal rotation; and dedicated display zones for statement pieces and accessories.
Lighting is a defining feature of luxury wardrobe systems. Warm-temperature LED strips (2700–3000K) positioned to illuminate garments without generating UV damage are standard in premium installations. The lighting serves both functional (colour accuracy) and experiential (theatrical reveal) purposes.
"A luxury wardrobe is designed twice: first for the garments it will hold, and second for the experience of inhabiting it."

IV · 02
Garment Preservation & Material Research
Garment Preservation Intelligence
Garment preservation is a discipline in itself — and one that is consistently undervalued in residential wardrobe design. Research into luxury garment care protocols reveals clear material-specific requirements that have direct implications for wardrobe configuration: wool and cashmere require horizontal folding (not hanging) to prevent distortion; silk garments must be kept from direct light exposure to prevent colour degradation; leather items require specific temperature and humidity ranges; shoes require cedar lasts and periodic conditioning.
Studio Materium's care intelligence layer was designed to surface this information at the item level — giving clients access to hanger type recommendations, lighting configuration, washing instructions, and storage methodology for every garment in their wardrobe. This transforms the platform from a configuration tool into an ongoing reference system.
Hanging
Wide-shoulder wooden hangers for structured jackets and coats. Padded satin hangers for silk. No wire hangers for any premium item.
Lighting
2700–3000K warm LED, max 3 lux at garment surface. UV-filtering lenses required for all natural fibres. No halogen.
Folding
Cashmere and heavy knits folded horizontally with acid-free tissue. No creasing at stress points. Maximum 3 garments per stack.
Washing
Material-specific wash icons. Cold gentle for silk and wool; dry clean for structured pieces. Visual washing table reference provided.
Material Categories in the Catalogue
Wool & Cashmere
Flat fold storage; cedar moth repellent; annual professional clean. Temperature: 16–20°C, 45–55% humidity.
Silk & Satin
Padded hanger; away from light; hand wash cold or dry clean. Store in breathable cotton bags.
Cotton & Linen
Machine washable (cold). Wide wooden hanger for structured pieces. Allow to air dry; press with steam iron.
Leather & Suede
Padded hanger; away from heat and moisture. Condition quarterly. Suede: brush after each wear. No plastic dust bags.
Synthetic & Technical
Machine wash per label. Avoid fabric softener on performance synthetics. Fold athletic items; hang structured.

IV · 03
Luxury Fashion Platforms — Digital Brand Systems
The visual language of luxury fashion digital platforms provides an important reference framework for this project. Brands including Net-a-Porter, Mr Porter, MatchesFashion, Mytheresa, and Farfetch have each developed distinctive digital identities that translate their editorial luxury positioning into interface design — with direct implications for how premium users expect digital products to look and behave.
Common conventions across these platforms include: near-black or white backgrounds with substantial negative space; editorial typography (often a classical serif for headings, a clean sans-serif for body); photography-forward layouts with minimal UI chrome; gold or earth-tone accent colours for premium tier differentiation; and sparse, purposeful animation — no gratuitous movement.
The interface hierarchy in these systems is consistently organised around product presentation, not navigation. The product card, the detail page, and the editorial lookbook are the primary visual units — the navigation is deliberately recessive, appearing only when needed. This principle — that the product should dominate, and the interface should serve — directly informed Studio Materium's design approach.
"In luxury digital design, restraint is the most powerful tool. What you remove is as important as what you include."
Platform Visual Language Analysis
Net-a-Porter / Mr Porter
Black-and-white editorial aesthetic. Serif headings (Caslon), sans-serif body. Product photography as primary visual currency. Recessive navigation. No decorative elements.
Mytheresa
Warm off-white backgrounds. Generous whitespace. Editorial curation via "Mytheresa Edits". Designer brand pages with distinctive typographic treatment.
Farfetch
More product-dense grid layout. Strong use of colour filtering and attribute-based navigation. "New Arrivals" editorial drop cadence. Slightly less editorial, more catalogue-oriented.
Insight for Studio Materium
The dark editorial aesthetic of Net-a-Porter/Mr Porter most closely aligns with Studio Materium's target user. The inky-black background, serif display typography, and restrained interaction model became direct reference points for this project's visual direction.

VII · 01
Design Philosophy
"Luxury is precision made visible. Every pixel is a decision."
Studio Materium's design system is built on a single organising principle: that a tool used by luxury professionals to serve luxury clients must itself feel luxurious. Not ornate — luxurious. The distinction matters. Ornamentation is the application of decorative elements to signal value. Luxury is the precision with which every element serves its purpose and nothing more.
The system is defined by three visual values. The first is Restraint: every element earns its place by serving a clear function. No decorative gradients, no drop shadows for effect, no animations that do not communicate state change. The second is Hierarchy: the typographic and spatial system creates unmistakable distinctions between display, heading, label, body, and micro text — so that the user always knows what level of information they are reading. The third is Precision: the 8-point spacing system, the sub-pixel typographic sizing, the exact colour values — nothing is approximate.
These three values produce a design language that communicates confidence without arrogance, sophistication without obscurity, and clarity without sterility.
Value 01 — Restraint
Remove everything that does not serve a function. The absence of an element is as powerful as its presence. Negative space is not empty space — it is structural.
Value 02 — Hierarchy
The user should never wonder what level of information they are reading, or what the most important element on the page is. Hierarchy makes complexity navigable.
Value 03 — Precision
Approximate is not acceptable. The spacing system, typographic scale, and colour values are defined and followed exactly. Precision is the signal of care.
Design Reference
Net-a-Porter editorial restraint · Architectural blueprint precision · Luxury watchmaking detail · Italian editorial photography spacing

Interaction Principles
No gratuitous animation
Every motion communicates something: state change, hierarchy, transition, or arrival. If the animation does not add information or aid comprehension, it is removed.
Immediate feedback
Every button press, approval action, and flag submission produces immediate visual feedback. The user should never wonder if their action was registered.
State is always visible
Approved items look different from flagged items look different from pending items — at a glance, with no scanning required. Colour and badge communicate state distinctly.
Destructive actions are confirmed
Delete item, remove from tray, and submit for review all require a confirmation step. Irreversible actions present a modal with clear CTA hierarchy: cancel (ghost) and confirm (primary).

IX · 01
Interactive Prototype
The Studio Materium prototype was built in two complementary forms. The first is a high-fidelity Figma interactive prototype, linked across all three user role flows — covering the complete Admin, Designer, and Client journeys with working navigation, modal interactions, and state transitions. The second is a live React 18 implementation deployed on Vercel, which was used for the primary user testing sessions.
The React prototype uses Zustand persist for state management, meaning that actions taken in one session persist across browser refreshes — allowing testers to experience the platform as a continuous workflow rather than a series of disconnected screens. Framer Motion animations were implemented throughout, providing the staggered list entries, modal transitions, and layout animations defined in the motion system.
The prototype supports all critical user flows: Admin project creation and designer assignment; Designer catalogue browsing, item selection, zone configuration, and submission; Client review, approval, flagging with notes, designer response viewing, and wardrobe finalization.

User Testing
Methodology
User testing was conducted over three days in May 2026, with five participants recruited across the three target roles. Sessions were moderated and conducted via screen share, with participants thinking aloud as they completed a set of predefined tasks on the live React prototype. Sessions were recorded with participant consent and lasted between 45 and 60 minutes each.
Each testing session began with a brief contextual scenario to orient the participant — "You are Priya Verma, a wardrobe designer at Studio Materium. You've just been assigned a new client project. Please complete the wardrobe configuration and submit it for review." — before the participant was given free control of the prototype.
Task success, time-on-task, error frequency, and verbal sentiment were recorded. Post-session, participants completed a brief satisfaction questionnaire covering ease of use, visual appeal, and task confidence.

Challenges & Future Scope
Challenges Faced
Aesthetic density vs. information clarity
The dark, editorial visual language creates an initial bias towards beauty over legibility. Maintaining readable information density while preserving the luxury aesthetic required constant calibration — especially on the project cards and item review rows where data is dense.
Designing for two drastically different tech literacies
The Designer persona (medium-high tech literacy, workflow-oriented) and the Client persona (low-medium, task-oriented) have fundamentally different interaction expectations. Creating an interface that serves both without compromising either required persistent user testing and iteration.
Scaling the catalogue system
The garment category hierarchy — Category → Sub-Category → Type → Variant → Gender → Occasion → Material — creates significant information architecture complexity. Designing a wardrobe builder that doesn't overwhelm while exposing this depth required multiple iterations of the navigation structure.
Maintaining luxury feel across functional states
Error states, loading states, empty states, and confirmation modals are inherently functional — and can easily feel at odds with a luxury aesthetic. Finding a visual language for these states that felt considered rather than utilitarian was a consistent design challenge.
Future Scope
AI Garment Recommendation Engine
A machine learning model trained on client preferences, occasion data, and wardrobe history that suggests additions, identifies gaps, and generates occasion-based outfit capsules automatically.
AR Wardrobe Spatial Preview
Augmented reality overlay that allows clients to visualize the configured wardrobe within their actual wardrobe space — showing garment placement, lighting effects, and spatial organisation.
Luxury Brand API Integration
Live product feed integration with Loro Piana, Max Mara, Brunello Cucinelli and similar brands — enabling designers to add items directly from brand catalogues with real-time pricing and availability.
Mobile Companion App
A native iOS app for clients — optimised for the low-tech literacy persona — allowing wardrobe review, approval, and care reference from a phone without requiring desktop access.
3D Wardrobe Spatial Preview
Interactive 3D visualization of the configured wardrobe space — allowing clients to walk through their wardrobe virtually before installation, with clickable garments revealing item details and care information.

X · 02 — Final Thoughts
A project that asked more of me
than I expected it to.
Studio Materium began as a practical design problem — how do you digitise a wardrobe curation workflow that currently exists in email threads and WhatsApp groups? — and became something significantly more complex and more rewarding than I anticipated.
The research phase confronted me with the reality that luxury is not a visual style. It is a quality of thought, applied consistently across every decision — from the information architecture to the choice of typeface weight to the exact milliseconds of a transition animation. Designing for users who interact with the highest-quality objects and environments every day means they will immediately notice the difference between a considered decision and an approximate one.
The user testing sessions were humbling in the best way. Watching P5 — a 58-year-old with no prior experience of workflow platforms — finalize a wardrobe in under 8 minutes after the refinements was the clearest possible signal that the interface was working. Not because it was simple, but because it was clear.
This project has given me a foundation in systems thinking, information architecture, design research, and luxury visual language that I intend to carry into a professional practice in UI/UX for premium brands. I am deeply grateful to my mentor Pragya Jain, to Studio Materium, and to the five people who gave their time and their honest attention to help me make it better.
